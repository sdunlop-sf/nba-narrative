The Problem Is Real — But the Framing Is Wrong

Customers are adopting AI faster than any technology in history. Australia has the highest adoption rate of ChatGPT among large English-speaking economies. It's changing expectations. Marketers are being asked how they can use it to solve real problems:

Retain in a crisis

Deliver the right below-the-line offers to retain customers in a cost-of-living environment. Precision, not volume.

Move faster

Deliver campaigns faster and with more variants. The old cadence of quarterly campaign planning is too slow for real-time signals.

Reduce waste

Optimise advertising spend to reduce wastage and improve ROAS. Every dollar that hits the wrong customer is burned.

Conversational, not broadcast

Meet customer expectations for two-way conversational interfaces. The era of no-reply emails and brochureware is ending.

Everyone is framing this as "add AI to your martech stack." That framing is how you get the failure rate:

88%
of AI pilots never make it to production — Beam, 2025

And Australia, despite high consumer adoption, also has the highest rate of scepticism around AI data handling and the strongest resistance to AI adoption for service among developed English-speaking nations. Your customers' customers are savvy and suspicious. You can't hack this together.

The actual problem isn't AI adoption. It's decisioning — who gets what offer, when, through which channel, and why. That's the hard part. The AI is just the engine.

The Evolution of Marketing Decisioning

I've lived through every era of this. From the client side, in the seat where these tools actually run. This is what the journey looks like:

Era 1 — The List Upload
Upload lists. Batch send.
Marketing was a spreadsheet and an email blast. Export from CRM, clean in Excel, upload to ESP, hit send. No targeting logic. No personalisation. The "decision" was whether to include someone on the list. Measured by open rates and gut feel.
Era 2 — The Journey Builder
Batch-loaded data. Journeys. Complex AMPScript.
Data got loaded into marketing platforms in batches. Journey builders let you create branching paths. AMPScript, SSJS, personalisation strings — increasingly complex logic embedded in templates. Better than batch-and-blast, but the "intelligence" was hand-coded conditional logic maintained by specialists. One person leaves, nobody can read the AMPScript.
Era 3 — The Decision Tree
Deterministic decision trees. Rules engines.
Platforms like Pega, SAS, and bespoke builds introduced formal decisioning — rules trees, eligibility matrices, champion/challenger testing. Real arbitration. Real contact policies. But maintained by specialist architects, living in proprietary rules languages, separated from the data, and taking months to implement. The right idea, the wrong era.
Era 4 — 2026
Moments that matter. Actions, not campaigns.
Data is unified. Reasoning is a platform capability. The unit of work shifts from "campaign" to "action" — a specific intervention, for a specific customer, at a specific moment. Deterministic at scale for the obvious decisions. Agentic reasoning at the edges for the ambiguous ones. Configured in days, not months. The system doesn't just execute — it reasons, observes, and proposes. This is what we're building.
Each era solved a real problem. Each era's solution became the next era's constraint. The question isn't "what tool do I add?" — it's "what does decisioning look like when data is unified and reasoning is possible?"

Why Pilots Die

This is the part I know from the practitioner side — not from reading about it, from living it.

Pilots die because they solve one channel, one segment, one campaign — and then can't scale. There's no arbitration across competing offers. No feedback loop from outcomes back to models. No auditability trail that compliance will accept. The model works in a notebook but there's no production wrapper.

An individual might be able to vibe-code their way to something by putting an LLM on top of a data lake. But enterprise financial services organisations need something more robust:

What a pilot looks like

  • One channel, one use case
  • Ad-hoc data pipeline
  • Model in a notebook
  • No contact policy
  • Manual offer selection
  • No audit trail
  • No feedback loop
  • Works for 3 months, then stalls

What production looks like

  • Omni-channel arbitration
  • Unified customer data platform
  • Registered, versioned models
  • Contact policy enforced
  • Scored, ranked, selected automatically
  • Full decision audit trail
  • Outcome → retrain → improve loop
  • Deployed in days, runs indefinitely

What Production-Grade Actually Looks Like

This is where Salesforce enters — but through the practitioner's lens, not the brochure.

01 Profiles with industry-specific data model for context

Data Cloud as the unified profile layer. Industry data model — not a generic CDP, a financial services data foundation. Eligibility, segments, features, customer state — all live where the data team already maintains it. No separate "decisioning data layer" with its own schema, its own ingest jobs, its own sync logic. The engine reads the data where it already lives.

02 Industry and task-specific tools with structured inputs and outputs

Einstein Studio as the scoring engine. One model per outcome, training on real campaign response signals. Agentforce as the action layer — structured functions, not open-ended LLM calls. The agent doesn't guess; it calls specific tools with guardrails. 15 registered GenAiFunctions, each with typed inputs, typed outputs, and deterministic behaviour.

03 Security and privacy across the board

Einstein Trust Layer for every LLM interaction. Data residency in the platform. Audit trails for every decision. Compliance-ready from day one — critical in Australian financial services where regulators are watching AI adoption closely. The scepticism stat isn't a problem to solve; it's a design constraint to honour.

04 Available seamlessly across every channel

These aren't components you assemble. They're a platform you configure. The decision runs the same logic whether the action executes in email, SMS, in-app, through an agent conversation, or via an API call to a third-party system. One arbitration engine, every channel.

Mode 1 + Mode 2

The core architectural idea: the right tool for the right decision.

Mode 1

Deterministic at scale

99% of decisions are not ambiguous. A customer hits a threshold, eligible actions are clear, one wins on a weighted score, a decision record is written. Sub-second, auditable, explainable, cheap. No LLM in the hot path. Fast and predictable.

Mode 2

Agentic at the edges

The 1% that are ambiguous — where two actions score within a confidence threshold, where the signal is novel, where the stakes are high — escalate. An agent reasons over the options with full context. Recommends a treatment, drafts the content, writes rationale. A human approves or overrides.

Neither mode is new on its own. Deterministic arbitration has existed for decades. LLM reasoning is everywhere now. The combination is new. Most platforms pick one: all deterministic (fast but brittle at the edges) or all agentic (flexible but slow and non-deterministic). This platform uses cheap math where math is enough, expensive reasoning where reasoning matters.

Where This Is Going — Agents and Apps

Today: packages that give you production decisioning in days. Next: agents that don't just execute decisions but compose them — within constitutional rails.

Once you have a reasoning layer in the platform, you can ask the system to reason about itself. Not just "which action for this customer?" but "which actions should even be in the catalogue?" and "which models are drifting?" and "which segments are changing behaviour?"

Every day, the system reviews its own performance. The operator's job shifts:

Today

  • Watch the dashboards
  • Spot the patterns
  • Make adjustments manually
  • Campaign-centric thinking

Tomorrow

  • Review the system's suggestions
  • Approve the ones that make sense
  • Override with domain expertise
  • Moment-centric, action-driven
The 88% fail because they try to go from zero to autonomous AI. The path that works is: platform first, then intelligence, then autonomy — each layer earning trust before the next one activates.

Honest Tensions

Credibility comes from naming the bets, not papering over them. These go on a slide, not buried in Q&A.

1. The deterministic core is good engineering, not a revolution.

Thin arbitration, weighted scoring, contact policy — this is what well-built decisioning has looked like for twenty years. The genuinely new part is the proactive agentic layer. Don't over-claim on the deterministic side. Own it as the correct modern re-implementation of a known pattern.

2. We are betting that reasoning stays expensive enough to keep deterministic in the hot path.

If inference costs drop 10x in 18 months — plausible — the "agentic everywhere" pitch gets more credible. The defence is auditability, explainability, unit economics at scale, compliance. Those hold even if LLMs get cheap.

3. The strongest contrarian view is real.

The maximalist argument: don't pre-define actions at all. Let an agent compose treatment at runtime, per customer, with tools. No catalogue, no arbitration, just reasoning + tools. Slower, pricier, less auditable — but can invent novel treatments. For regulated enterprise we win. For digital-native high-velocity shops, that pitch lands better than ours. Know which segment the room is in.

4. Australia's AI scepticism is a feature, not a bug.

The highest scepticism rate of AI data handling among English-speaking nations means the audience is pre-disposed to distrust "just add AI" messaging. Lead with governance, auditability, and human-in-the-loop. This audience rewards rigour over hype.

"I want to name what we're betting on and what we're not claiming, because if I just stand here and tell you our architecture is self-evidently correct you should not trust me." — turns the talk from a pitch into a thesis, and makes the audience lean in.

Telling the Story — Five Beats

Start with the evolution they've lived. Build to the problem. Land on the proof.

Walk through the four eras. Every marketer over 30 just nodded at eras 1 and 2. Every architect nodded at era 3. Now you've earned permission to tell them what era 4 looks like.

"Everyone is adopting AI. Australia is leading among English-speaking countries for consumer adoption. But here's the thing — 88% of AI pilots never make it to production. Practical Tips for the Talk

  • Open with the evolution, not architecture. Walk them through the four eras — they'll see themselves in the story before you go technical.
  • The 88% stat is your anchor. Return to it: "That's how you get the other 12%."
  • Don't say "I'm the SME." Show it. The side project proves you can build. The Salesforce build proves you can govern. Both together are the credential.
  • The vibe-code confession is your trust moment. You're not selling against the audience's instincts — you're sharing them. "I did this too. Here's what I learned." Then pivot.
  • Lead with Australian context. The scepticism stat makes the audience feel seen, not sold to. It signals you've done the homework.
  • "Days, not months" should land at least twice. Once in the architecture section, once in the proof.
  • Name the honest tensions. "If I just stand here and tell you our architecture is self-evidently correct, you should not trust me." The room leans in.
  • Don't name competitors. When asked "how is this different from X?" say "the comparison misses the point. X was designed for a different problem in a different era."
  • Close with the frontier, not the roadmap. "Platform first, then intelligence, then autonomy" is the hook.
  • The SVP wants speed-to-value. Deliver it through the proof, not the slides. Show the deployed package, the test count, the setup agent. That's the talk landing.
