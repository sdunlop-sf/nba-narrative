A Note First

Thank you for the rigour and transparency you brought to the CDP evaluation. The process was well-run, the feedback was direct, and we genuinely appreciated the opportunity.

We respect the decision and we're not writing to re-open it. What we did want to share, now that we've had time to reflect properly, is a conversation we didn't have during the process. Not a feature we missed. A different problem entirely.

The Conversation We Didn't Have

The CDP evaluation was about data routing: moving event data reliably from your app to Braze, AppsFlyer, and your analytics stack. On that question, a purpose-built CDP is the right answer, and the decision makes sense.

The conversation we didn't have is what happens after the data arrives. Which clients do you act on, and how do you decide? That's not a CDP problem. That's a decisioning problem, and the data you need to solve it isn't in your marketing stack.

It's in Financial Services Cloud. Your client data: trading behaviour, account activity, service history, relationship status. It's already in Salesforce. You don't need to move it, migrate it, or rebuild it. You just need to make it intelligent.

Today, the decisioning logic that acts on your clients lives in Braze: segments, journey conditions, send rules — built and maintained by the marketing team. It works. But it only sees campaign data. It doesn't see that a client's trading frequency has dropped for three consecutive weeks, that they raised a support case last month, or that their account balance has declined below a threshold that historically precedes churn. Those signals are in FSC. They're not reaching the decisioning layer.

What Intelligent Looks Like

Einstein Studio, trained on CMC's own client outcome data inside FSC, scores every client continuously: churn risk, propensity to increase activity, likelihood to respond to an intervention. Those scores don't just change what Braze sends. They determine whether a client gets a campaign, a call from a relationship manager, or a service workflow, all triggered from the same model on the same client record.

The model learns from commercial outcomes, not campaign opens. Every client who churned after a signal was missed, every intervention that worked, becomes a training signal. The next recommendation is better than the last. That's the compounding return that rules-based logic, or any tool that only sees your marketing data, cannot deliver.

Layer Role
FSC The signal source: trading activity, service history, relationship data, account status. Already captured. Already governed.
Einstein Studio The decisioning engine, trained on CMC's own outcome data. Scores every client on churn risk, activity propensity, intervention priority. Learns continuously.
Agentforce The action layer. Triggers the right response: a campaign in Braze, a task for a relationship manager, a service workflow. Auditable, explainable, governed.
mParticle / Braze Unchanged. The decisioning output flows in as a signal. Same tools, better instructions.
A Word on the Alternatives

Braze's BrazeAI Decisioning Studio (formerly OfferFit) gives them a strong native AI layer: reinforcement learning that continuously optimises which message, offer, and timing performs best for each individual inside the Braze platform. It's genuinely good at what it does.

What it sees is campaign data. What it optimises is marketing actions. It doesn't have visibility of trading behaviour, account status, service interactions, or relationship history, and it can't trigger a relationship manager workflow or create a case in FSC.

Braze AI makes your campaigns smarter. Salesforce decisioning makes your client relationships smarter. For a trading platform where the decisions that matter most happen outside the marketing stack, that distinction is significant.

The same logic applies to building a decisioning layer in a data warehouse, whether that's BigQuery, Snowflake, or Databricks. A model that lives there can score a client. Getting it there requires pulling FSC data out through an ETL pipeline, which introduces latency and governance overhead. And once the model has a score, something still has to push it back into FSC to create a relationship manager task, trigger a service workflow, or update the client record. That's a second integration problem on top of the first.

We've seen this play out with other customers. A next best action model built in a data warehouse, passed to a CDP, passed again to downstream channels for rendering. Each hop introduces latency. Each handoff is a point where the decision and the asset can fall out of sync. The client receives an offer in one channel that no longer reflects what the model decided, because something changed between the score and the render. For asset management and trading platforms where trust and precision matter, that inconsistency has a direct cost. The data you need for client decisioning lives in FSC. A model that runs natively on that data, and acts natively on that platform, doesn't have either problem.

Different buyer, different budget

This is a retention and commercial conversation, not a marketing infrastructure one. Different stakeholders, different value case, different approval path.

A single use case, client churn risk scoring, can be scoped as a POC with an agreed success metric before any broader platform decision is made.

Runs alongside mParticle

This doesn't compete with the CDP decision. It operates above it, and the output feeds directly into Braze as a decisioning signal.
